Living through rising heat

India's cities are getting hotter. But what thermometers record is often very different from what human bodies feel through the day and during nights.

The year 2024 was the hottest ever recorded, and 2025 followed close behind as the third warmest year on Earth since 1850, data from the World Meteorological Organization shows.

In fact, years since 2015 form the hottest decade on record, with temperatures remaining dangerously high. In the three-year period from 2023–2025, for the first time, the average global temperature crossed 1.5 °C above pre-industrial levels — a key climate threshold scientists have long warned about.

On April 26, 2026, all of the world's top 50 hottest cities were in India. Across the 50 cities, the average peak temperature that day was 44.7 °C. Even the coolest maximum on the list — Solapur at 41.9 °C — “would be considered a public health emergency anywhere in Europe”. At 46.2 °C, Banda in the northern state of Uttar Pradesh recorded the world's highest temperature that day.

A report by Harvard University found just 8% of households in India have access to air conditioning. Others rely on passive cooling — shade, reflective roofs, cross-ventilation. Around three-fourths of the country's workforce is engaged in agriculture and construction. Informal and gig workers constitute as much as 90% of the labour force, leaving many without contracts and protections, according to the paper from the Salata Institute's Climate Adaptation in South Asia research cluster.

The Indian Meteorological Department officially declares a heatwave when the maximum temperature crosses 40 °C in the plains, 37 °C along the coast, or 30 °C in the hills, and is at least 4.5 °C above the seasonal normal. But the lived experience of heat-stress runs far ahead of these thresholds. Once humidity, low wind, and direct sun are factored in, what a body actually feels at 40 °C can sit closer to 48–52 °C on the UTCI “felt” scale — the point at which the body can no longer shed heat fast enough to keep its core temperature stable.

The consequences show up across the day. Outdoor labour — on farms, construction sites, delivery routes, brick kilns — slows or stops in the afternoon hours; productivity drops sharply once felt-temperature crosses the “strong heat stress” band (UTCI > 32 °C), and becomes hazardous beyond 38 °C. Children miss school. Pregnancies are higher-risk. Hospital admissions for cardiac and renal stress climb. And at night, when cities once cooled enough for the body to recover, “tropical nights” (T2M > 25 °C through the night) cut sleep short, raise resting heart rate, and compound the next day's exposure. The thermometer says “heatwave” only some of the time. The body is in heat-stress for a lot longer.

How to read: the y-axis is “days” — total hours in the year above the threshold, divided by 24. So a value of 10 means a cumulative 10 × 24 hours of exposure across the year, regardless of which days or hours those came from.

Thresholds (UTCI — felt temperature, solid line):

  • Strong heat stress — UTCI > 32 °C
  • Very strong heat stress — UTCI > 38 °C
  • Extreme heat stress — UTCI > 46 °C
  • Tropical nights — T2M > 25 °C during IST night hours (7:30 PM – 7:30 AM). This panel is based on actual air temperature, not UTCI.

Dotted overlay: “days of actual temperature” counts hours where T2M (air temperature at 2 m) crossed the same threshold, divided by 24. The gap between solid and dotted is the heat amplification from humidity, wind and radiation.

Source: Copernicus Thermal Trace (ERA5 hourly T2M and derived UTCI). Hourly UTC samples re-interpreted to IST during computation. See INSIGHT-METHOD.pdf for full definitions and formulas.
