Living through rising heat

India's cities are getting hotter. But what thermometers record is often very different from what human bodies feel through the day and during nights.

2024 was the hottest year on record, globally and in India, according to the World Meteorological Organization and NASA. But the temperatures recorded on thermometers tell only part of the story. The heat people actually experience is shaped not just by air temperature, but also by humidity, wind, sunlight, and how little relief arrives after sunset. Across India's cities, days are becoming more stressful, nights are staying warmer for longer, and the body is increasingly exposed to heat that feels far more intense than the numbers alone suggest.

The number of days with heat stress across cities has gone up

Counting how many day-equivalents per year a city crosses each heat-stress threshold — and how often nights stay tropical. Hourly exposure is summed across the year and expressed in “days” (hours ÷ 24), so 10 means a cumulative 10 × 24 hours above the threshold.

How to read: the y-axis is “days” — total hours in the year above the threshold, divided by 24. So a value of 10 means a cumulative 10 × 24 hours of exposure across the year, regardless of which days or hours those came from.

Thresholds (UTCI — felt temperature, solid line):

  • Strong heat stress — UTCI > 32 °C
  • Very strong heat stress — UTCI > 38 °C
  • Extreme heat stress — UTCI > 46 °C
  • Tropical nights — T2M > 25 °C during IST night hours (7:30 PM – 7:30 AM). This panel is based on actual air temperature, not UTCI.

Dotted overlay: “days of actual temperature” counts hours where T2M (air temperature at 2 m) crossed the same threshold, divided by 24. The gap between solid and dotted is the heat amplification from humidity, wind and radiation.

Source: Copernicus Thermal Trace (ERA5 hourly T2M and derived UTCI). Hourly UTC samples re-interpreted to IST during computation. See INSIGHT-METHOD.pdf for full definitions and formulas.
